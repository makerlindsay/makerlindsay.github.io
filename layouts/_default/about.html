Lindsay's professional identity is rooted in her background as a creative educator — an approach that transforms leadership from a top-down management style into a participatory art form.

Her foundation was built in the heart of the "Maker" movement. As a Business Advisor and Product Developer at Chibitronics, she navigated the complexities of the customer journey — bridging the gap between vision and execution, spearheading the restructuring of the digital buying experience, and leading cross-functional teams to build a comprehensive Educational Resource Hub.

By translating user needs into actionable user stories, she learned how to create containers that are both functional and inspiring.

With a career spanning over a decade, she bridges the gap between technical infrastructure and human connection — helping groups move from fragmentation to coherence.

Leadership Methodology

Five pillars — and the
deeper story behind each

Lindsay's identity translates her professional qualities into a specific methodology for group leadership. Each pillar is more than a service — it's a distinct philosophy shaped by decades of practice.

01
The Gathering — Leadership through Invitation
Focus: Clarity & Coherence

In the corporate world, gatherings are often mandatory and transactional. Lindsay reimagines this by focusing on human-centered leadership. She uses her background in educational strategy to ensure that every participant knows why they are there — creating a "felt sense of belonging" that allows for deeper collective focus without the pressure of forced intensity.

02
The Container — Mastering the "Hold"
Focus: Emotional & Relational Safety

Building on her experience managing complex maker spaces, Lindsay treats the "container" as both a physical and emotional infrastructure. She excels at regulating pace — slowing a group down when they are rushing through discomfort, or speeding them up when they are stuck. This is critical for groups navigating integration after intense experiences.

03
The Ceremony — Secular Meaning-Making
Focus: Presence over Belief

Lindsay de-mystifies ceremony, making it a tool for community milestones. By rooting ritual in "presence rather than belief systems," she makes meaning-making accessible to diverse, modern teams. This pillar is about marking the threshold moments — the start of a new project, or the closing of a significant chapter. Ceremony is how groups remember what they're doing together.

04
Meaning Made Together — Shared Authorship
Focus: Participatory Structures

This pillar is the direct evolution of her Maker philosophy. Instead of a leader delivering a message, Lindsay designs structures where the group "makes" the meaning through collaborative art, ritual, or reflection. It shifts the power dynamic from one-to-many to many-to-many — where every participant is a contributor, not a spectator.

05
Workshops for Meaningful Making — Structured Exploration
Focus: Insight & Expression

These are the tactical application of her work. Her workshops are "living containers" — responsive to the room's energy. Unlike static seminars, these sessions use creative expression and storytelling to help individuals find personal clarity while contributing to the collective's goals. They don't just teach — they transform.

Personal Background

Between
many worlds,
fully present

Lindsay was born in Michigan, grew up in South Korea for seven and a half years, and carries her Puerto Rican heritage with her everywhere. She has spent her adult life between San Francisco and Stockholm — two cities that have shaped her practice in complementary and unexpected ways.

This is not a story of instability. It is a story of radical belonging — of someone who learned early that home is not a fixed place, but a quality of attention you bring to wherever you are.

That understanding is the root of everything she does. Whether she is facilitating a community retreat or holding the close of a ceremony, Lindsay brings the same quality: a genuine, unhurried presence that makes people feel seen.

"I've never had just one place. So I learned to be fully present in whichever place I'm in — and to help others do the same."
Core Philosophy

Presence is the practice.
Belonging is the outcome.

Lindsay believes that the quality of a gathering is determined long before anyone arrives. It lives in the questions asked before the invite is sent, in the structure designed to invite rather than demand, and in the quality of attention the facilitator brings into the room.

She does not believe in forced connection or manufactured intensity. She believes in creating the conditions for what already wants to happen — and then holding steady while it does.

"I don't create for people. I create with them."
